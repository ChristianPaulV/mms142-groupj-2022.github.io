Beyond Generation

Peek at the larger picture and the future of AI Image Generation

On AI Image Generation
vs Professionals

Would AI-generated art be considered a different category of art? A different art style? Would this open doors to people who don’t find themselves to be artistically inclined but would know a good artwork when they see one?   An article featured an AI-generated image winning a competition. The winner was also interviewed and featured in the news.
Next 9NEWS (2022): AI-generated art piece takes first at the state fair
When Jason M. Allen, the winner of the competition, posted his three art entries online, it garnered a lot of protests from artists. They seem to claim that he cheated, that he was not an artist as it was made by a robot, likening it to a “Banana taped to a wall”. Allen disagrees, saying that he put a lot of work and effort into his art and that it wasn’t an easy process at all, claiming to put in over 80 hours of work into it, even needing to edit the final drafts before he could submit them as entries (Metz, 2022).

Would this mean there would be an emergence of different kinds of artists? One that is neither traditional nor digital but something else entirely? An artist who uses AI-generated images from prompts and others to create artwork—an AI artist? A prompt engineer?

But are they really artists if they didn’t do the heavy lifting that the AI is doing? There really isn’t such a term as “AI artists” because these people aren’t considered artists at all. If they aren’t artists, should their “works” be considered art? Would AI-generated art create an entirely new category in the art world?

Another article featured an artist who created a children’s book through the use of artificial intelligence tools, ChatGPT, MidJourney, and others. It garnered a bit of backlash from people, saying that he was plagiarizing, questioning his expertise in writing children’s books, and even claiming that the whole book was not his work as it was all made by an AI. At the end of the article, the artist in question did acknowledge the concerns that various people had about the tools he used to create his work (Endicott, 2022).

Apart from the implication of AI supposedly competing with human artists and the perceived threat to the job security of artists, there is also the ethical sphere that we may have to contend with. An example that garnered the concern of many was the incident wherein the style of the late artist Kim Jung Gi, well known for his photographic memory and freehand-inked works, was fed into an AI Image Generator to produce works that imitate Kim Jung Gi’s style. The backlash could be attributed to how untimely or distasteful the act was as the famous artist had only recently passed away when the AI-generated images were released. However, many artists, big or small, fear their works are being unceremoniously referenced by AI without their permission, which they view as “ art theft ”(Foley, 2022).

This point could be counter-argued that a human artist similarly references others too when creating an illustration/image, as renowned American filmmaker Jim Jarmusch says that “Nothing is original.” Adam Duff, better known as LUCIDPIXUL, argues that while the use of AI in itself is harmless, it is said that only when AI image generation is used to “hijack” the identity of an artist is it when it truly “crosses the line” (Duff, 2022).

Duff insists that while an artist cannot claim or legally enforce an art style, the style of an artist is tied to their own identity. When an artist admires a master he/she/they would imitate or study the master’s work, as it made an impression on the artist. Can we say that the artist is engaging in unethical behavior? If we abide by Duff’s statement then the answer would be no (Duff, 2022).This back and forth between the pros and the nay-sayers can go on for eternity, as such the ethical concern will be a pressing matter while a consensus is still being debated.

On Copyright Infringement and Intellectual Property Rights

Concerning copyright infringement and Intellectual Property rights (IP), a few important questions have to be addressed.
There are users on various social media platforms concerned with “art theft”, as AI-generated images are taking aspects of their and others’ artworks and “mixing” it all together to form a singular artwork.

Do AI Image Generators “steal?”
There are users on various social media platforms concerned with “art theft”, as AI-generated images are taking aspects of their and others’ artworks and “mixing” it all together to form a singular artwork.

Using the wealth of information present in our discussion of AI Image Generation , data that is curated by the developers of an AI tool to make a dataset—sometimes including images that you post online—are used as training data to for the AI Image Generator to create entirely new images from scratch, i.e., a novel image.

In short, AI Image Generators do not steal existing artworks but instead, use them as inspiration in the way that a human artist would. At the end of the day, AI is supposed to mimic human behavior.

However, cases of art theft could technically occur if a dataset that is fed onto an AI Image Generator contains copyrighted images. For example, Plunkett (2022) discussed a situation wherein an AI Image Generator is fed with copyrighted images that have their watermarks and signatures erased. Cases like this argue for the need for regulation of AI Image Generation to prevent further damage both to its users and AI as a whole.

Are AI-generated images protected by Copyright infringement?
Copyright protection only applies to human authors. By the letter of the law, one cannot claim copyright protection for an AI because the AI is a non-human entity and is not recognized as a legal person. Therefore, the question then becomes should the copyright extend to the user who “created” the artwork through the use of an AI image generator? But then again, who gets credited for the artwork?

Epstein et al. (2020) tackled this set of questions and their answer was “it’s complicated”. The results of the research were not straightforward and there was no solid designation of who owned the art or not but a “hierarchy of accountability” was provided.

At the top of the hierarchy is the artist (person who takes inspiration/references/samples from the crowd and creates art using the generative AI software), then followed by the technologist (person who created the software), and finally the crowd (people who create the inspiration/references/samples that the artist uses).

Who owns the copyright for AI-generated artwork?
It would depend on the country where the art was created as each handles copyright protection differently. It is actually not unusual for the copyright owner to be different from the creator of the work.


According to an article by Marchi (2022), copyright is determined on a case-by-case basis: 1. Work for hire. Since works that are made within the duties of the employment or under a specific agreement belong to the employer/commissioner (unless otherwise agreed), the copyright usually belongs to the client or company who the work was made for. 2. Assignment/license of copyright. The copyright owner may sell or license the rights to someone else through an assignment. This often occurs with musicians who are under contract with major record labels, or with writers and publishing houses. 3. No Copyright Owner.  There may have been no copyright at all, or the copyright may have expired (to the so-called public domain). AI-generated work, however, has a tendency to not be protected by copyright due to a present lack of regulations (as with all new/breakthrough technologies). The two solutions offered to this problem are to treat AI as either Work for hire or, if that option is not possible, treat the AI-generated images as a product for the consumer.

Regardless, however, this issue about IP needs to be officially addressed at some point. Preferably as soon as possible as it seems that AI-generated content will continue to become more popular, especially among big enterprises.

On Sexualized AI-Generated Images

The problem with making AI-generated art is the fact that anyone can use it, and technology isn’t infallible. Even with filters, we can see that people are able to bypass the safety filters of Stable Diffusion thanks to this Reddit post from the creators themselves.

Art theft and invasive data gathering are certainly ethically concerning when it comes to AI Image Generation, but none is more concerning and more pressing than the fact that these images can be sexualized. It does not even have to be with open-source apps like Stable Diffusion. Lensa’s AI was caught generating NSFW content with images from users who were unaware of their likenesses being used in such works (Tangalakis-Lippert, 2022). It even came to a point where One user’s childhood photos were used in creating such content (Snow, 2022).

While there are filters and restrictions for such images to be generated, it does not seem to be heavily enforced or regulated which is concerning as such things could become issues that the media could exaggerate when left unchecked (Vincent, 2022)— which would have an impact on the company/brand/creator of the application and possibly, in general, the creative freedom of users online.

On Art Theft and Bad Actors

The ethical ramifications of AI-generated images are concerning and their potential to change society, its laws, and bylaws around the internet, and Netiquette is huge. Jiang (2022) presented an article wherein an art thief “captured” an ongoing Livestream of an artist’s painting, then proceeded to use an AI Image Generator to finish the work before the artist did so.

A repeat of a similar situation could be an influential factor for artists to eventually avoid posting WIPs (work-in-progress art) or streaming their work in the future. Thankfully, the thief did not get away with it, but it has still set a precedent in the art world.

The following speculative situations may also occur:

• Streaming one’s work is one of the few ways an artist can interact with their audience and patrons. Changing Livestreaming behavior could effectively remove this line of communication between artist and audience.
• A start of a movement consisting of artists encouraging other artists to archive their art streams if they have any (i.e., change in artistic process). If this becomes a demand in the future, new applications or social media websites—that are dedicated to recording and archiving artwork—could pop up.

On NEXT-GEN Artificial Intelligence

The following section will speculate the future of development in Artificial Intelligence.

For future enterprise and consumer applications, there are at least three possible ways in which AI tools will be embedded. It can be either through standalone AI products that work under a cloud-processing model (similar to Adobe Creative Cloud) or as individual AI tools implemented as features into already popular image/video processing tools (such as what AI-enabled features Blender is fast incorporating into its menu options). A third option, which is already happening but will become more sophisticated, is for AI platforms to allow AI-enhanced browsers (ergo, the wider public) to access its functions.

Still beyond these “just-around-the-corner” tools, visionary AI developers are also aiming for AI-powered operating systems, and AI-powered features as well within application suites that are not specifically just for image/video processing but for a wider range of user needs (e.g. imagine an AI-powered Microsoft Office or AI-powered Google Maps/Google Earth).

With these predictions, further questions can be asked such as which AI startups can be the “disruptors?” Will OpenAI, forerunners in the explosive race among AI Image Generators, remain as the “to-be disrupted?” Until when before another disruptive innovation will heavily affect AI (similar to the Internet giving AI developers more accessible and larger datasets)? Out of all these questions, perhaps the most important one is how will AI tools further develop in terms of acquiring more functionalities.

To answer the last question, it is imperative to take note of a September 2022 discussion between the CEO of OpenAI, Sam Altman, with Hoffman Reid.

The 2022 discussion of Reid and Altman begins with the OpenAI CEO describing AI as a “genuine technological revolution” consisting of tools to “significantly augment human ideas and workflows on an everyday basis.” Altman proceeded to describe the successful AI models as a “foundational platform” from which numerous advancements would be made “across all industries [followed by] an explosion of new companies.”

In other words, Altman is referring to AI’s crucial role across the board, in all kinds of derivative technologies and applications (Reid & Altman, 2022 Sep 13). But certainly the sudden mushrooming of AI art generators just this year (2022), and the enthusiastic response of millions of users (not just artists) getting on board within the span of a few months, is enough validation of his claim—this is not a flash in the pan.

Altman also likened the overall impact of AI (including AI Image Generators) to the introduction and popularization of cellular mobile phone usage in the 1990s (Reid & Altman, 2022 Sep 13)—which is too modest of a comparison. The AI Text-to-Image revolution should rather be compared to that of the global explosion of the Internet from the 1990s onward.

Finally, in discussing the future of AI tools, Altman sees “massive leaps forward” in at least two areas for improvement in general AI capabilities, both of which will clearly fuel more advances in AI Image Platforms: (1) in large language models, and (2) in “multimodal models that move between image and language.”

Large language models, such as OpenAI’s GPT family, are crucial because they allow millions of Internet users (many of them with no familiarity with the likes of Adobe software) to use natural language, almost to loosely chat with the GPT and, based on that interaction, to give iterative instructions or suggestions to the AI image platform, causing it to generate amazing arrays of images and variations of images. The resounding success of platforms such as MidJourney, Dall-E, and Stable Diffusion, all rely on these large language models (Reid & Altman, 2022 Sep 13).

For Altman, Language models in the near future will use better and better algorithms, even potentially to the point where its AI capabilities may even outrun the availability of fresh data and higher computational power (Reid & Altman, 2022 Sep 13).

For the concept of multimodal models, Altman also foresees AI models with the capacity to shift more seamlessly from one multimedia mode to another. “We will get true multimodal models working—not just text and images but every modality you have in one model is able to easily fluidly move between things” (Reid & Altman, 2022 Sep 13). In fact, developers in the field of 3D Augmented Reality and Virtual Reality (3D AR/VR)—now often called Extended Reality or XR—are already seeing the arrival of AI components packaged as “autonomous smart agents” (virtual humans or humanized objects) in the round, with which we humans can interact not just via text and images, but via speech, facial and bodily gestures, and other modalities.

As early as during the SIGGRAPH Asia workshop in 2017, the question was already posed and discussed: "what would happen when artificial worlds start to think, and how we humans can interact and communicate with AI through e.g. affective interfaces?" (Lugmayr et al., 2017).

By 2021, a systematic review of recent tech literature revealed that “36 published records containing AI-XR combinations” indicated the use of such specific combinations in medical and surgical training, autonomous vehicles and robotics, military applications, gaming environments, and entertainment (Reiners et al., 2021).

On AI and Creative
Industries as a whole

Technological innovations in art: parallels in recent (19th and 20th-century) history
Just limiting ourselves to the visual arts, we can cite at least four well-known technologies that revolutionized the way we as human societies record, represent, and enrich our lives through visual media that we value for its cultural-aesthetic as well as functional-artisan content and form.

These are (a) photography, (b) cinema/video, (c) basic digital image/video processing, and (d) online/interactive multimedia. In each case, the core of these technologies were clearly disruptive innovations in what we can now collectively refer to as “creative industries”. They were disruptive, not just in reshaping the creative businesses and professions whose careers and incomes derived from these fields, but also in reshaping the general public’s artistic and consumer habits and tastes. As we step on the threshold of the AI-art era, it is instructive to review these earlier technologies.

a. Photography. Before photography, the dominant fields of visual media were painting, sculpture, and related arts and crafts, which included engraving, lithography, and more recent printing processes. Photography, at first a curiosity practiced by early 19th-century nerds, soon created its own industrial behemoths and artistic/consumer products and tastes. As photography became more accessible, especially in tandem with the print industries, its associated products, professions, and businesses eventually replaced (or marginalized, or pushed into specialized niches) the formerly more prominent types of visual arts and crafts. Once color photography tied up with glossy high-quality printing processes, the works of the masters–from Mesopotamia to the Mona Lisa to Mondrian–were almost as enjoyable to the ordinary public as to Louvre tourists.

b. Cinema and video . Before long, photography branched off into film/cinema, then video. This new set of technological innovations created yet previously unheard-of artistic/consumer products and tastes, and newer professions and business behemoths that catered to the demand. Again, as cinema and video became part of powerful mass media, they replaced (or marginalized, subsumed, or tied up with) such similar but older modes of cultural expression as theater, concerts, and other live spectacles (e.g. circuses, traveling shows). Hollywood, a sleepy outback town in the early 20th century, came to represent the global power of cinema and the cultures and lifestyles it represented.
d. Early 21st-century innovations . From the 2000s onwards, we have seen a series of massive explosions in which advances in digital tech played a huge role in overhauling visual arts and modes of expression, via: e.  Basic digital image/video processing. Throughout the 1970s-1990s, digital tech began to show its potential to revolutionize visual media and arts in various ways. Small and separate streams of innovation gradually converged into what became a gigantic river by the late 1990s. • Public access to online multimedia (e.g. YouTube, TikTok, cloud computing, massive online databases);
• Cinematic extensions via motion capture, realistic 3D animation, miniature cameras built into drones, etc.
• Initial use of AI-enhanced functions embedded into smart digital tools, many of them open-source (e.g. Blender)
• Massive expansion in ways that visual products are commodified, distributed, and consumed: cinema SFX, streaming-video pay sites (e.g. Netflix), ultra-realistic video games blending into VR, online libraries of high-res photos and 3D models, even so-called “non-fungible tokens” (NFTs).
• The capacity to convert analog to digital images via optical scanners.
• Direct-to-digital production via graphics applications (e.g. Corel and Adobe) and digital cameras.
• High resolutions, more efficient formats, smart filters, and higher-capacity PCs, digicams, storage media, and displays and printers, that could enhance, manipulate and display digital images and video with better results.
One result, as most of us know by now, is that photography and videography are no longer the exclusive turfs of highly trained professionals with specialized gear, but a common arena of creativity where anyone with a decent cellphone camera and editing app can compete.

The parallels that we see in these four giant leaps of technology affecting visual media and arts, we think, are logically and historically connected enough to expect a fifth giant leap to generate similar impacts in the creative industries, in the general public’s consumer-artistic tastes and habits, and indeed, in the value assigned by entire societies to artistic work in general.

    Medium-term impacts of AI innovations on the
    creative industries

    As we tried above to retrace the interaction of technology and art, we now realize more fully that the human artistic creative process is both intimately personal and deeply social in character. Artists don’t live isolated in ivory towers or insulated in a vacuum, but as part of the wider society where they are affected by the multi-faceted impacts of technology, economics, politics, and culture.

    There is some basis for the growing concern that AI-driven art tools will destroy the personal touch and the artists’ work as individuals (which was, by the way, a value enshrined by the Renaissance but not in prior eras). There is indeed the risk that these AI tools would turn the creative process into a big profit machine controlled by big business (which in many fields today it already is, anyway). But there are also the new potentials of the same creative process becoming more socialized if not collectivized in character, while small innovative groups and individuals use the same AI tools to remain independent and competitive.

    Generally, technology (especially modern information technology) empowers society to maximize its many modes of communication and self-expression. Society’s creative output is thereby increased multiple times. Formerly elitist art becomes an arena that more people can engage in, enjoy, and share. Anyone with access to AI tools today can become an “instant artist” – in much the same way that 10-20 years ago, anyone with access to a digital camera could become an “instant photographer/videographer.” (Also, in roughly similar positions, did the early photographers become “instant painters”, to the chagrin of professionally acclaimed painters.)

    But will the tsunami of massively produced AI art cheapen the value of art in general or the specific works of individual artists? Even tech-savvy digital artists who have joined the growing debate on this issue.

    What is definite, however, is that there will be growing pains that must be promptly addressed, at least minimized and remedied if not fully resolved. These include violations of intellectual property rights (IPR), of rules against plagiarism, and laws and ethical standards on pornography and identity forgery as these are currently defined and widely observed.

    As seen in our review of past innovations, the growth in art production fuelled similar growth in its economic aspects:

    • more people (with varied skills) and businesses (of varying size) engage in art production for their incomes or profit;
    • more art-laden products turn into commodities and command their own prices, reflecting high demand;
    • businesses engaged in art production and distribution (what we now call the creative industries) proliferate to respond to the fast-growing demand;
    • consumer-artistic habits and tastes are influenced by the new products, even reshaped drastically by the success of certain creative works (especially when pushed by dominant cultural forces and corporate advertising).

    In other words, value chains grow and become more elaborate within the main creative industries and in related professions, if only because art production becomes more sophisticated, and now involves more technology and collective/collaborative work. Just think how many in-house art production crews and outsourced art services are involved in the production of a single Marvel or DC blockbuster.

    Reid and Altman (2022, Sept 13) explored the probable restructuring of industries impacted by AI as a whole, particularly in the use of AI models and platforms:

    • There will be “a small handful of fundamental large [AI] models” that will allow APIs to be built on top of them.
    • There will be a middle layer of startup AI-based companies that don’t need to “reinvent the wheel” (i.e., develop and train their own AI models) but use these existing APIs to create specialized versions of the same AI models to create new products and services of long-lasting value.
    • Altman believes that small startups should leverage the advantage of low costs and fast cycle times. "And if you have those, you can then compete as a startup against the big incumbents."

    Impact on the broader visual arts (including design and scientific, practical applications)

    There is often a tendency to equate the entirety of visual arts with (or have it represented by) the more aesthetic disciplines or genre (aka “fine arts”), as taught in art school, subjected to critical reviews, displayed in galleries, or at least created based on some high artistic standards and therefore expected to command a higher price for its intrinsic value.

    The other branches are often forgotten or deemed marginal. I.e., “I’m an artist, you’re in layout design, s/he’s just an effin’ Photoshop/Autocad implementor or grunt for an animation scene.”

    But let’s constantly remind ourselves that, throughout history, art has never really been separable from crafts (the production of materially useful goods and services), which in turn require certain degrees of personal and team skills—the result of which is that much of the visual arts created by society are not displayed in museums and galleries, not viewed in theaters and sold at auctions, but are embedded in other mundane things that we use every day: buildings (via architecture), appliances, furniture and wearables (via industrial and fashion design), books and magazines (layout), etc.

    Many areas of pre-digital creative media and arts—from crumbling artifacts of ancient cultures to recent analog libraries and artworks—are being given new leases on life and wider access by a huge global public with the help of digital visualization tools. AI is increasingly used to automate these otherwise tedious and error-prone archival/restoration processes. Examples that are clear in our chosen topic of AI-generated art are enhancement/restoration and colorization of old photographic images and films.

    Image depicting an AI enhancement/restoration and colorization.
    Even in the natural and social sciences, the role of such practical applications of the digital art technology is increasingly recognized, e.g., the rapid growth of data visualization and computerized simulation/reconstruction and model-making (in biochemistry, physics including astrophysics, medicine, anthropology, archeology, legal forensics, etc). All these are being created, not just by data scientists and computer geeks, but by a new generation of scientists/technicians trained in data visualization, which is fast developing its own principles of digital aesthetics and AI-friendly methods.And we haven’t even begun to speak yet of still more branches of digital technology that provide increasingly smarter tools not just for visualization on computer screens and printouts, but physical/virtual interaction in full 3D. Ultra-realistic video games in 3D AR/VR environments are becoming useful tools in education, training, and research. 3D printing has its own explosive growth both for the supply side and the demand side. And in the reverse direction, which some theorists think will also become increasingly important, we humans will soon be conversing and creatively collaborating with AI “smart agents” via such 3D AR/VR environments – for example, in designing a home, doing a virtual 3D “walkthrough”, and giving instructions to our AI Archi-designer helper to “move this post a bit to the right, have smaller windows on the left, and so on.”The purpose of pointing these out, we think, is to further emphasize that the technological revolution brought by AI in visual arts is not limited to very specific (and easily recognized) fields where a visual work can stand alone as art, but in so many products, services, and processes where the role of the visual-creative aspect is embedded and hidden. AI art technology may thus be seen as a very generalized tool with so many potential applications that for now are still unknown or just being discovered. These potential innovations and uses will certainly create new generations of “disruptor” startups in the creative industries (which are themselves already exploding). These disruptors could well mostly be small indie groups and individuals, but also possibly renegade innovators working quietly within the established giants.
