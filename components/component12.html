There is often a tendency to equate the entirety of visual arts with (or have it represented by) the more aesthetic disciplines or genre (aka “fine arts”), as taught in art school, subjected to critical reviews, displayed in galleries, or at least created based on some high artistic standards and therefore expected to command a higher price for its intrinsic value.

The other branches are often forgotten or deemed marginal. I.e., “I’m an artist, you’re in layout design, s/he’s just an effin’ Photoshop/Autocad implementor or grunt for an animation scene.”

But let’s constantly remind ourselves that, throughout history, art has never really been separable from crafts (the production of materially useful goods and services), which in turn require certain degrees of personal and team skills—the result of which is that much of the visual arts created by society are not displayed in museums and galleries, not viewed in theaters and sold at auctions, but are embedded in other mundane things that we use every day: buildings (via architecture), appliances, furniture and wearables (via industrial and fashion design), books and magazines (layout), etc.

Many areas of pre-digital creative media and arts—from crumbling artifacts of ancient cultures to recent analog libraries and artworks—are being given new leases on life and wider access by a huge global public with the help of digital visualization tools. AI is increasingly used to automate these otherwise tedious and error-prone archival/restoration processes. Examples that are clear in our chosen topic of AI-generated art are enhancement/restoration and colorization of old photographic images and films.
