When Jason M. Allen, the winner of the competition, posted his three art entries online, it garnered a lot of protests from artists. They seem to claim that he cheated, that he was not an artist as it was made by a robot, likening it to a “Banana taped to a wall”. Allen disagrees, saying that he put a lot of work and effort into his art and that it wasn’t an easy process at all, claiming to put in over 80 hours of work into it, even needing to edit the final drafts before he could submit them as entries (Metz, 2022).

Would this mean there would be an emergence of different kinds of artists? One that is neither traditional nor digital but something else entirely? An artist who uses AI-generated images from prompts and others to create artwork—an AI artist? A prompt engineer?

But are they really artists if they didn’t do the heavy lifting that the AI is doing? There really isn’t such a term as “AI artists” because these people aren’t considered artists at all. If they aren’t artists, should their “works” be considered art? Would AI-generated art create an entirely new category in the art world?

Another article featured an artist who created a children’s book through the use of artificial intelligence tools, ChatGPT, MidJourney, and others. It garnered a bit of backlash from people, saying that he was plagiarizing, questioning his expertise in writing children’s books, and even claiming that the whole book was not his work as it was all made by an AI. At the end of the article, the artist in question did acknowledge the concerns that various people had about the tools he used to create his work (Endicott, 2022).

Apart from the implication of AI supposedly competing with human artists and the perceived threat to the job security of artists, there is also the ethical sphere that we may have to contend with. An example that garnered the concern of many was the incident wherein the style of the late artist Kim Jung Gi, well known for his photographic memory and freehand-inked works, was fed into an AI Image Generator to produce works that imitate Kim Jung Gi’s style. The backlash could be attributed to how untimely or distasteful the act was as the famous artist had only recently passed away when the AI-generated images were released. However, many artists, big or small, fear their works are being unceremoniously referenced by AI without their permission, which they view as “ art theft ”(Foley, 2022).

This point could be counter-argued that a human artist similarly references others too when creating an illustration/image, as renowned American filmmaker Jim Jarmusch says that “Nothing is original.” Adam Duff, better known as LUCIDPIXUL, argues that while the use of AI in itself is harmless, it is said that only when AI image generation is used to “hijack” the identity of an artist is it when it truly “crosses the line” (Duff, 2022).

Duff insists that while an artist cannot claim or legally enforce an art style, the style of an artist is tied to their own identity. When an artist admires a master he/she/they would imitate or study the master’s work, as it made an impression on the artist. Can we say that the artist is engaging in unethical behavior? If we abide by Duff’s statement then the answer would be no (Duff, 2022).

This back and forth between the pros and the nay-sayers can go on for eternity, as such the ethical concern will be a pressing matter while a consensus is still being debated.
